Between the time your offer is accepted and the closing date there is a lot going on. Here are some of the things that will be happening over the coming weeks.

  • Appraisal - Your lender will want to have an appraisal on the home you plan to buy. Your new home is the collateral for your loan so they want to make sure its value justifies the amount you are borrowing.
  • Credit Report - If you haven't pre-qualified for a loan, your lender will obtain a credit report for you to determine your credit score and what loan terms you qualify for.
  • Title Search - This is an investigation of ownership history of the property. It also a check to see if any liens or tax liabilities exist. Again, the lender wants to make sure their loan to you won't entangle them in an ownership dispute.
  • Homeowner's Insurance - You will need to open an insurance policy on your new home, you will need to provide proof of insurance to the lender.
  • Inspection - The lender may not require this but you should take the time and spend the money on a professional home inspector to make sure the property is in sound condition structurally. You don't want to move into a home only to find that there are major problems (like a sagging roof or termite damage) that will be expensive to repair and lower the property value or cause such a financial burden on you that you default on the loan.
  • Closing Funds - You may be required to bring a certified bank check, instead of a personal check, to the closing to cover the various closing fees. Your lender should give you an exact amount a few days before the closing.

Most of these processes will be arranged by your lender but you can request to be present (such as a home inspection) or given the results before the closing. Unless you've made arrangements for the seller to pay for these services they will be part of the closing fees you will pay.
